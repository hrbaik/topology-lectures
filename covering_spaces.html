Introduction to Covering Space Theory

Harry Hyungryul Baik (KAIST)

0. Motivation: From Poincaré to Coverings

The Big Picture

Recall the Poincaré Conjecture (now a theorem, proved by Perelman in 2003): every compact, simply connected 3-manifold is homeomorphic to $S^3$.

Why do topologists care so much about classifying simply connected spaces? One deep reason: every "nice" space has a simply connected space sitting above it, called its universal cover. This universal cover determines the topology of the original space almost completely, up to the action of the fundamental group.

In other words, if we understand simply connected spaces, then — through covering space theory — we can understand all spaces (of a given type), because every such space is a quotient of its universal cover by a group action. This is the motivation for this lecture: let us learn the theory that makes this precise.

0.1   Review: The Fundamental Group

Definition: Fundamental Group

Let $X$ be a topological space and $x_0\in X$ a basepoint. A loop based at $x_0$ is a continuous map $\gamma\colon [0,1]\to X$ with $\gamma(0) = \gamma(1) = x_0$.

Two loops $\gamma_0, \gamma_1$ are homotopic (relative to endpoints) if there exists a continuous map $H\colon [0,1]\times[0,1]\to X$ with $$H(s,0) = \gamma_0(s),\quad H(s,1) = \gamma_1(s),\quad H(0,t) = H(1,t) = x_0 \;\;\text{for all } s,t.$$

The fundamental group $\pi_1(X,x_0)$ is the set of homotopy classes $[\gamma]$ of loops, with group operation given by concatenation: $$[\gamma]\cdot[\delta] \;=\; [\gamma * \delta], \qquad (\gamma*\delta)(s) = \begin{cases}\gamma(2s) & 0\le s\le \tfrac{1}{2},\\ \delta(2s-1) & \tfrac{1}{2}\le s\le 1.\end{cases}$$ The identity is the class of the constant loop, and inverses are given by $[\gamma]^{-1} = [\bar\gamma]$ where $\bar\gamma(s) = \gamma(1-s)$.

Definition: Simply Connected

A path-connected space $X$ is simply connected if $\pi_1(X,x_0) = \{e\}$ (the trivial group) for some (hence every) basepoint $x_0$.

Equivalently, $X$ is simply connected if every loop in $X$ can be continuously shrunk to a point.

0.2   The Key Idea

From Universal Covers to Classification

Here is the paradigm that covering space theory establishes:

Given a path-connected, locally path-connected, semi-locally simply connected space $B$:

1. There exists a universal covering space $\widetilde{B}\to B$, which is simply connected.

2. The fundamental group $\pi_1(B)$ acts on $\widetilde{B}$ by deck transformations, freely and properly discontinuously, and $B \cong \widetilde{B}/\pi_1(B)$.

3. Every connected covering space of $B$ corresponds to a conjugacy class of subgroups of $\pi_1(B)$.

So knowing $\widetilde{B}$ (a simply connected space) and $\pi_1(B)$ (a group) completely determines the topology of $B$ and all its coverings. This is why the Poincaré conjecture — a statement about simply connected spaces — has such far-reaching consequences.

0.3   The Prototypical Example

$\mathbb{R} \to S^1$

The map $p\colon\mathbb{R}\to S^1$ given by $p(t) = e^{2\pi i t}$ is the prototypical example. $\mathbb{R}$ is the universal cover of $S^1$; it is simply connected. The fundamental group $\pi_1(S^1)\cong\mathbb{Z}$ acts on $\mathbb{R}$ by integer translations: $n\cdot t = t+n$. The quotient $\mathbb{R}/\mathbb{Z}$ recovers $S^1$.

The intermediate covers are $S^1\xrightarrow{z\mapsto z^n} S^1$, corresponding to the subgroups $n\mathbb{Z}\le\mathbb{Z}$. The larger the subgroup, the "smaller" the covering space.

1. Covering Spaces

1.1   Definition

Definition: Covering Map

Let $E$ and $B$ be topological spaces. A continuous surjection $p\colon E\to B$ is a covering map (and $E$ is a covering space of $B$) if every point $b\in B$ has an open neighborhood $U$ such that $$p^{-1}(U) \;=\; \bigsqcup_{\alpha\in A} V_\alpha$$ is a disjoint union of open sets $V_\alpha\subseteq E$, each mapped homeomorphically onto $U$ by $p$.

Such a $U$ is called evenly covered. The sets $V_\alpha$ are called sheets over $U$.

Immediate Properties

$p$ is a local homeomorphism. Each $V_\alpha\to U$ is a homeomorphism, so around every point $e\in E$ there is an open set mapped homeomorphically to an open set in $B$.

$p$ is an open map. If $W\subseteq E$ is open and $e\in W$, find a sheet $V_\alpha$ containing $e$; then $p(W\cap V_\alpha) = p|_{V_\alpha}(W\cap V_\alpha)$ is open in $U$, hence in $B$.

The fiber $p^{-1}(b)$ is discrete. Each sheet $V_\alpha$ contributes exactly one point to $p^{-1}(b)$, and the $V_\alpha$ are disjoint open sets. If $B$ is connected, the cardinality $|p^{-1}(b)|$ is constant; this is the number of sheets (or degree) of the covering.

1.2   Key Examples

Example 1: $\mathbb{R}\to S^1$

$p(t) = e^{2\pi it}$. The real line $\mathbb{R}$ wraps around the circle infinitely many times. Every open arc $U\subsetneq S^1$ is evenly covered: $p^{-1}(U)$ is a countably infinite union of disjoint open intervals in $\mathbb{R}$, each mapped homeomorphically onto $U$. This is an infinite-sheeted covering.

Example 2: $S^1\to S^1$, $z\mapsto z^n$

For each integer $n\ge 1$, the map $p_n(z) = z^n$ (thinking of $S^1\subseteq\mathbb{C}$) is an $n$-sheeted covering. The fiber over any point $w$ is $p_n^{-1}(w) = \{z\in S^1 : z^n = w\}$, which consists of exactly $n$ equally spaced points.

Example 3: $S^n\to\mathbb{R}P^n$

The sphere $S^n$ is a double cover of real projective space $\mathbb{R}P^n$ via the quotient map $p(x) = [x]$ that identifies each $x$ with $-x$. Every fiber has exactly $2$ points. Since $S^n$ is simply connected for $n\ge 2$, this is the universal cover of $\mathbb{R}P^n$, and $\pi_1(\mathbb{R}P^n)\cong\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$.

Example 4: Product Coverings

If $p\colon E\to B$ and $p'\colon E'\to B'$ are covering maps, then $p\times p'\colon E\times E'\to B\times B'$ is a covering map. For instance, $\mathbb{R}^2\to T^2$ (given by $(s,t)\mapsto (e^{2\pi is}, e^{2\pi it})$) is the universal cover of the torus.

1.3   Deck Transformations

Definition: Deck Transformation

Let $p\colon E\to B$ be a covering map. A deck transformation (or covering transformation) is a homeomorphism $\phi\colon E\to E$ such that $p\circ\phi = p$, i.e., $\phi$ permutes the fibers.

The set of all deck transformations forms a group under composition, denoted $\mathrm{Deck}(E/B)$ or $\mathrm{Aut}(p)$.

Examples of Deck Groups

$\mathbb{R}\to S^1$: The deck transformations are the integer translations $t\mapsto t+n$ for $n\in\mathbb{Z}$. So $\mathrm{Deck}(\mathbb{R}/S^1)\cong\mathbb{Z}$.

$S^n\to\mathbb{R}P^n$: The only nontrivial deck transformation is the antipodal map $x\mapsto -x$. So $\mathrm{Deck}(S^n/\mathbb{R}P^n)\cong\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$.

$S^1\xrightarrow{z^n}S^1$: The deck transformations are rotations by $2\pi k/n$, i.e., $z\mapsto e^{2\pi ik/n}z$. So $\mathrm{Deck}\cong\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$.

Proposition: Deck Transformations are Determined by One Point

If $E$ is connected and $p\colon E\to B$ is a covering map, then a deck transformation $\phi$ is uniquely determined by the image of a single point. That is, if $\phi(e_0) = \psi(e_0)$ for some $e_0\in E$, then $\phi = \psi$.

Proof

Let $S = \{e\in E : \phi(e) = \psi(e)\}$. We show $S$ is both open and closed in $E$; since $E$ is connected and $S\ni e_0$ is nonempty, $S = E$.

$S$ is open. Let $e\in S$, so $\phi(e) = \psi(e) = e'$. Choose an evenly covered neighborhood $U$ of $p(e)$ and let $V$ be the sheet containing $e'$. Then $\phi^{-1}(V)\cap\psi^{-1}(V)$ is an open neighborhood of $e$ on which $\phi$ and $\psi$ agree (both map into $V$, and $p\circ\phi = p\circ\psi = p$ forces equality within a single sheet).

$S$ is closed. We show $E\setminus S$ is open. Let $e\in E\setminus S$, so $\phi(e)\ne\psi(e)$. Let $U$ be an evenly covered neighborhood of $p(e) = p(\phi(e)) = p(\psi(e))$, with $p^{-1}(U) = \bigsqcup V_\alpha$ a disjoint union of sheets. Since $\phi(e)\ne\psi(e)$ but $p(\phi(e))=p(\psi(e))$, the two points lie in different sheets: $\phi(e)\in V_\alpha$ and $\psi(e)\in V_\beta$ with $V_\alpha\cap V_\beta=\emptyset$. By continuity of $\phi$ and $\psi$, there is an open neighborhood $W$ of $e$ with $\phi(W)\subseteq V_\alpha$ and $\psi(W)\subseteq V_\beta$. Since $V_\alpha$ and $V_\beta$ are disjoint, $\phi(w)\ne\psi(w)$ for every $w\in W$. So $W\subseteq E\setminus S$, proving $E\setminus S$ is open. □

2. Lifting Theorems

The Central Question

Given a covering map $p\colon E\to B$ and a continuous map $f\colon Y\to B$, does there exist a lift $\tilde{f}\colon Y\to E$ with $p\circ\tilde{f} = f$?

$$\begin{array}{ccc} & & E \\ & \overset{\tilde{f}}{\nearrow} & \downarrow\, p \\ Y & \xrightarrow{f} & B \end{array}$$

The lifting theorems give precise conditions under which such lifts exist and are unique. They are the engine of covering space theory.

2.1   Unique Lifting Property

Theorem (Unique Lifting Property)

Let $p\colon E\to B$ be a covering map, $Y$ a connected space, and $f\colon Y\to B$ continuous. If $\tilde{f}_1, \tilde{f}_2\colon Y\to E$ are two lifts of $f$ (i.e., $p\circ\tilde{f}_1 = p\circ\tilde{f}_2 = f$) that agree at some point $y_0\in Y$, then $\tilde{f}_1 = \tilde{f}_2$.

Proof

The argument is identical to the uniqueness proof for deck transformations. Let $S = \{y\in Y : \tilde{f}_1(y)=\tilde{f}_2(y)\}$.

$S$ is open. For $y\in S$, let $e = \tilde{f}_1(y) = \tilde{f}_2(y)$. Choose an evenly covered $U\ni f(y) = p(e)$ and the sheet $V$ containing $e$. Then $\tilde{f}_1^{-1}(V)\cap\tilde{f}_2^{-1}(V)$ is an open neighborhood of $y$. On this set, both $\tilde{f}_1$ and $\tilde{f}_2$ map into $V$ and satisfy $p\circ\tilde{f}_i = f$, so they agree (since $p|_V$ is injective).

$S$ is closed. For $y\in\overline{S}$, the same sheet argument shows $\tilde{f}_1(y)$ and $\tilde{f}_2(y)$ must lie in the same sheet, hence are equal.

So $S$ is clopen, nonempty (contains $y_0$), and $Y$ is connected, giving $S = Y$. □

2.2   Path Lifting

Theorem (Path Lifting)

Let $p\colon E\to B$ be a covering map, $\gamma\colon [0,1]\to B$ a path, and $e_0\in E$ with $p(e_0) = \gamma(0)$. Then there exists a unique lift $\tilde\gamma\colon[0,1]\to E$ with $\tilde\gamma(0)=e_0$ and $p\circ\tilde\gamma = \gamma$.

Proof

Uniqueness follows immediately from the Unique Lifting Property (with $Y=[0,1]$, connected).

Existence. For each $t\in[0,1]$, choose an evenly covered neighborhood $U_t$ of $\gamma(t)$. The collection $\{\gamma^{-1}(U_t)\}_{t\in[0,1]}$ is an open cover of the compact space $[0,1]$.

By the Lebesgue number lemma, there exists $\delta \gt 0$ such that every subinterval of $[0,1]$ of length $\le\delta$ is mapped by $\gamma$ into some $U_t$. Choose a partition $0 = t_0 \lt t_1 \lt \cdots \lt t_N = 1$ with $t_{j+1}-t_j \lt \delta$.

We build the lift inductively. On $[t_0,t_1]$: $\gamma([t_0,t_1])\subseteq U$ for some evenly covered $U$. Let $V$ be the sheet over $U$ containing $e_0$. Define $\tilde\gamma = (p|_V)^{-1}\circ\gamma$ on $[t_0,t_1]$. This is continuous, starts at $e_0$, and satisfies $p\circ\tilde\gamma = \gamma$.

At step $j$: suppose $\tilde\gamma$ is defined on $[0,t_j]$. The point $\tilde\gamma(t_j)$ lies in $E$ with $p(\tilde\gamma(t_j)) = \gamma(t_j)$. Choose an evenly covered $U'$ containing $\gamma([t_j,t_{j+1}])$ and the sheet $V'$ containing $\tilde\gamma(t_j)$. Extend $\tilde\gamma$ on $[t_j,t_{j+1}]$ by $\tilde\gamma = (p|_{V'})^{-1}\circ\gamma$. The extensions match at $t_j$ by construction.

After $N$ steps, $\tilde\gamma$ is defined on all of $[0,1]$. □

2.3   Homotopy Lifting

Theorem (Homotopy Lifting Property)

Let $p\colon E\to B$ be a covering map, and let $H\colon[0,1]\times[0,1]\to B$ be a continuous map (a homotopy). Given a lift $\tilde{H}_0\colon[0,1]\to E$ of $H(\cdot,0)$ (i.e., $p\circ\tilde{H}_0 = H(\cdot,0)$), there exists a unique lift $$\widetilde{H}\colon[0,1]\times[0,1]\to E \quad\text{with}\quad \widetilde{H}(s,0) = \tilde{H}_0(s) \;\text{ for all } s, \quad p\circ\widetilde{H} = H.$$

Proof

Uniqueness follows from the Unique Lifting Property with $Y = [0,1]\times[0,1]$ (connected) and the two lifts agreeing on $[0,1]\times\{0\}$.

Existence. The proof extends the path-lifting argument to two dimensions.

Step 1 (Grid). For each $(s,t)\in[0,1]^2$, choose an evenly covered neighborhood $U_{s,t}$ of $H(s,t)$. The preimages $\{H^{-1}(U_{s,t})\}$ form an open cover of $[0,1]^2$. By the Lebesgue number lemma (applied to the compact metric space $[0,1]^2$), there exists $\delta \gt 0$ so that every square of side $\le\delta$ is mapped by $H$ into some evenly covered set. Choose partitions $$0 = s_0 \lt s_1 \lt \cdots \lt s_M = 1, \qquad 0 = t_0 \lt t_1 \lt \cdots \lt t_N = 1$$ with mesh $\lt\delta$.

Step 2 (Induction). We lift $H$ on the subrectangles $R_{ij} = [s_i,s_{i+1}]\times[t_j,t_{j+1}]$ in lexicographic order: first fill row $j=0$ (using $\tilde{H}_0$), then row $j=1$, etc.

For each rectangle $R_{ij}$: $H(R_{ij})$ lies in some evenly covered $U$. The lift is already defined on the bottom edge (from the previous row) and the left edge (from the previous column, or the initial condition). Since $R_{ij}$ is connected, the previously lifted boundary forces us into a unique sheet $V$ over $U$. Define $\widetilde{H} = (p|_V)^{-1}\circ H$ on $R_{ij}$.

Step 3 (Consistency). Adjacent rectangles share an edge on which the lift is already defined, and both constructions agree on this edge by the uniqueness of lifts on connected sets. So the piecewise definition glues to a continuous map on all of $[0,1]^2$. □

2.4   Consequences of Homotopy Lifting

Corollary: Lifts of Homotopic Paths Share Endpoints

Let $p\colon E\to B$ be a covering map. If $\gamma_0,\gamma_1\colon[0,1]\to B$ are path-homotopic (rel endpoints) and $\tilde\gamma_0,\tilde\gamma_1$ are their lifts starting at the same point $e_0\in p^{-1}(\gamma_0(0))$, then $\tilde\gamma_0(1)=\tilde\gamma_1(1)$.

Proof

Let $H$ be the homotopy from $\gamma_0$ to $\gamma_1$ rel endpoints. Lift $H$ to $\widetilde{H}$ with $\widetilde{H}(s,0)=\tilde\gamma_0(s)$. By uniqueness, $\widetilde{H}(\cdot,1)$ is the unique lift of $\gamma_1$ starting at $\tilde\gamma_0(0)=e_0$, so $\widetilde{H}(\cdot,1) = \tilde\gamma_1$.

Now consider the path $t\mapsto\widetilde{H}(1,t)$ in $E$. Applying $p$: $p(\widetilde{H}(1,t)) = H(1,t) = \gamma_0(1)$ for all $t$ (since $H$ is a homotopy rel endpoints). So this path lies entirely in the fiber $p^{-1}(\gamma_0(1))$. But the fiber is discrete and $[0,1]$ is connected, so a continuous map from $[0,1]$ into a discrete set must be constant. Therefore $\tilde\gamma_1(1) = \widetilde{H}(1,1) = \widetilde{H}(1,0) = \tilde\gamma_0(1)$. □

Corollary: The Induced Homomorphism $p_*$ is Injective

If $p\colon (E,e_0)\to (B,b_0)$ is a covering map, the induced homomorphism $$p_*\colon\pi_1(E,e_0)\to\pi_1(B,b_0)$$ is injective.

Proof

Suppose $p_*([\tilde\gamma]) = [p\circ\tilde\gamma] = [c_{b_0}]$ (the constant loop at $b_0$). Then $p\circ\tilde\gamma$ is homotopic to the constant loop at $b_0$. The constant loop at $b_0$ lifts to the constant loop at $e_0$. By the previous corollary, $\tilde\gamma$ and the constant loop at $e_0$ share the same endpoint, which is $e_0$.

Now lift the homotopy between $p\circ\tilde\gamma$ and $c_{b_0}$: we get a homotopy between $\tilde\gamma$ and $c_{e_0}$ in $E$. So $[\tilde\gamma] = [c_{e_0}]$ is trivial. □

Significance

The injectivity of $p_*$ means $\pi_1(E,e_0)$ embeds as a subgroup of $\pi_1(B,b_0)$. The image $p_*\bigl(\pi_1(E,e_0)\bigr)\le\pi_1(B,b_0)$ is the subgroup of loops in $B$ whose lifts starting at $e_0$ are again loops (i.e., return to $e_0$). This subgroup will be the key to classifying coverings (Section 3).

2.5   The General Lifting Criterion

Theorem (Lifting Criterion)

Let $p\colon (E,e_0)\to (B,b_0)$ be a covering map, and let $f\colon (Y,y_0)\to (B,b_0)$ be continuous with $Y$ path-connected and locally path-connected. Then a lift $\tilde{f}\colon (Y,y_0)\to (E,e_0)$ with $p\circ\tilde{f}=f$ exists if and only if $$f_*\bigl(\pi_1(Y,y_0)\bigr) \;\subseteq\; p_*\bigl(\pi_1(E,e_0)\bigr).$$ Moreover, if a lift exists, it is unique.

Proof

Uniqueness. This is the Unique Lifting Property: $Y$ is connected, and any two lifts agree at $y_0$, so they agree everywhere.

($\Rightarrow$) If $\tilde{f}$ exists, then $f = p\circ\tilde{f}$, so $f_* = p_*\circ\tilde{f}_*$, hence $\mathrm{Im}(f_*)\subseteq\mathrm{Im}(p_*)$.

($\Leftarrow$, Construction) For $y\in Y$, choose a path $\alpha$ in $Y$ from $y_0$ to $y$. Then $f\circ\alpha$ is a path in $B$ from $b_0$ to $f(y)$. By the Path Lifting Property, lift it to $\widetilde{f\circ\alpha}$ starting at $e_0$. Define $$\tilde{f}(y) \;=\; \widetilde{f\circ\alpha}(1).$$

Well-definedness. Suppose $\beta$ is another path in $Y$ from $y_0$ to $y$. We must show the lifts of $f\circ\alpha$ and $f\circ\beta$ end at the same point. Consider the loop $\alpha*\bar{\beta}$ at $y_0$. Its image $f\circ(\alpha*\bar{\beta})$ is a loop at $b_0$ whose class $[f\circ(\alpha*\bar{\beta})]$ lies in $f_*(\pi_1(Y,y_0)) \subseteq p_*(\pi_1(E,e_0))$ by hypothesis. So $[f\circ(\alpha*\bar{\beta})] = p_*([\tilde{\gamma}])$ for some loop $\tilde{\gamma}$ in $E$ at $e_0$. This means the loop $f\circ(\alpha*\bar{\beta})$ lifts to a loop at $e_0$. But the lift of $f\circ(\alpha*\bar{\beta})$ is the concatenation of the lift of $f\circ\alpha$ (ending at $\widetilde{f\circ\alpha}(1)$) followed by the lift of $f\circ\bar{\beta}$ (which, run backwards, starts at $\widetilde{f\circ\beta}(1)$). Since the whole lift is a loop, it returns to $e_0$, forcing $\widetilde{f\circ\alpha}(1) = \widetilde{f\circ\beta}(1)$.

Continuity. Let $y\in Y$. Let $U$ be an evenly covered neighborhood of $f(y)$ in $B$, and let $V$ be the sheet of $p^{-1}(U)$ containing $\tilde{f}(y)$, so that $p|_V\colon V\to U$ is a homeomorphism. Since $Y$ is locally path-connected, there exists a path-connected open neighborhood $W$ of $y$ with $f(W)\subseteq U$.

For any $y'\in W$: choose a path $\alpha$ from $y_0$ to $y$ (used to define $\tilde{f}(y)$), and let $\delta$ be a path from $y$ to $y'$ inside $W$. Then $\alpha*\delta$ is a path from $y_0$ to $y'$, and $$\tilde{f}(y') = \widetilde{f\circ(\alpha*\delta)}(1).$$ The lift of $f\circ(\alpha*\delta)$ is: first the lift of $f\circ\alpha$ (ending at $\tilde{f}(y)\in V$), then the lift of $f\circ\delta$ starting at $\tilde{f}(y)$. Since $f(\delta)$ lies entirely in $U$ and the lift starts in the sheet $V$, the lift of $f\circ\delta$ stays in $V$ (because $p|_V$ is a homeomorphism onto $U$). Therefore $\tilde{f}(y') \in V$, and in fact $$\tilde{f}(y') = (p|_V)^{-1}\bigl(f(y')\bigr) \quad\text{for all } y'\in W.$$ Thus $\tilde{f}|_W = (p|_V)^{-1}\circ f|_W$, which is a composition of continuous maps, hence continuous. Since $y$ was arbitrary, $\tilde{f}$ is continuous. □

2.6   Application: $\pi_1(S^1)\cong\mathbb{Z}$

Setup

We apply the lifting theorems to the covering map $p\colon\mathbb{R}\to S^1$, $p(t)=e^{2\pi it}$, with basepoints $\tilde{x}_0 = 0\in\mathbb{R}$ and $x_0 = 1\in S^1$. The fiber $p^{-1}(x_0) = \mathbb{Z}\subset\mathbb{R}$.

The Lifting Correspondence

Define a map $\phi\colon\pi_1(S^1,x_0)\to\mathbb{Z}$ as follows. Given a loop $\gamma$ in $S^1$ at $x_0$, lift it to a path $\tilde{\gamma}$ in $\mathbb{R}$ starting at $0$. The endpoint $\tilde{\gamma}(1)$ lies in $p^{-1}(x_0) = \mathbb{Z}$. Set $$\phi([\gamma]) = \tilde{\gamma}(1).$$

Theorem

$\phi\colon\pi_1(S^1,x_0)\to\mathbb{Z}$ is an isomorphism of groups.

Proof

Well-definedness. If $\gamma\simeq\gamma'$ rel endpoints, the Homotopy Lifting Property lifts the homotopy to a homotopy $\widetilde{H}$ in $\mathbb{R}$ with $\widetilde{H}(0,t)=0$ for all $t$. By the Unique Lifting Property applied to the paths $s\mapsto\widetilde{H}(1,s)$ (each a lift of the constant path at $x_0$, starting at $\tilde{\gamma}(1)$ when $s=0$ and at $\tilde{\gamma}'(1)$ when $s=1$), we get $\tilde{\gamma}(1)=\tilde{\gamma}'(1)$. Alternatively: $\widetilde{H}(\cdot,0)=\tilde{\gamma}$ and $\widetilde{H}(\cdot,1)=\tilde{\gamma}'$ are both lifts starting at $0$; the map $t\mapsto\widetilde{H}(1,t)$ is a continuous map $[0,1]\to\mathbb{Z}$ (discrete), hence constant, so $\tilde{\gamma}(1)=\tilde{\gamma}'(1)$.

Homomorphism. Let $\gamma,\delta$ be loops at $x_0$. Lift $\gamma$ to $\tilde{\gamma}$ starting at $0$, with $\tilde{\gamma}(1)=m$. Lift $\delta$ to $\tilde{\delta}$ starting at $0$, with $\tilde{\delta}(1)=n$. Now consider the concatenation $\gamma*\delta$: its lift starts at $0$, follows $\tilde{\gamma}$ to $m$, then continues with a translate of $\tilde{\delta}$. Precisely, $t\mapsto\tilde{\delta}(t)+m$ is a lift of $\delta$ starting at $m$ (since $p(s+m)=p(s)$ for any integer $m$). So the lift of $\gamma*\delta$ ends at $m+n$. Hence $\phi([\gamma]*[\delta])=m+n=\phi([\gamma])+\phi([\delta])$.

Surjectivity. For each $n\in\mathbb{Z}$, the path $\tilde{\omega}_n(t)=nt$ in $\mathbb{R}$ projects to a loop $\omega_n = p\circ\tilde{\omega}_n$ in $S^1$ at $x_0$, and $\phi([\omega_n])=\tilde{\omega}_n(1)=n$.

Injectivity. Suppose $\phi([\gamma])=0$, i.e., the lift $\tilde{\gamma}$ starts and ends at $0$. Then $\tilde{\gamma}$ is a loop in $\mathbb{R}$ at $0$. Since $\mathbb{R}$ is simply connected, $\tilde{\gamma}\simeq c_0$ rel endpoints. Composing with $p$ gives $\gamma = p\circ\tilde{\gamma}\simeq p\circ c_0 = c_{x_0}$, so $[\gamma]$ is trivial. □

The Generator

The loop $\omega_1(t)=e^{2\pi it}$ (one counterclockwise traversal of $S^1$) generates $\pi_1(S^1)\cong\mathbb{Z}$. The loop $\omega_n$ that winds $n$ times represents the class $n\in\mathbb{Z}$. In particular, $\omega_1$ and $\omega_{-1}$ are not homotopic — the direction of traversal matters.

3. The Universal Cover and Classification

3.1   The Universal Covering Space

Definition: Universal Cover

A covering map $p\colon\widetilde{B}\to B$ is a universal covering if $\widetilde{B}$ is simply connected.

Equivalently (by the Lifting Criterion), $\widetilde{B}$ is universal because it covers every connected covering of $B$: if $q\colon E\to B$ is any connected covering, there exists a covering map $r\colon\widetilde{B}\to E$ with $q\circ r = p$.

Proposition: Why "Universal"

Let $p\colon(\widetilde{B},\tilde{b}_0)\to(B,b_0)$ be the universal covering, and $q\colon (E,e_0)\to (B,b_0)$ any connected covering. Then there exists a covering map $r\colon\widetilde{B}\to E$ with $q\circ r = p$. In other words, the universal cover covers every other connected covering.

Proof

Step 1 — Existence of $r$. Since $\widetilde{B}$ is simply connected, $p_*(\pi_1(\widetilde{B},\tilde{b}_0)) = \{e\}$. This is trivially contained in $q_*(\pi_1(E,e_0))$. By the Lifting Criterion (with $Y=\widetilde{B}$, $f=p$, and the covering $q\colon E\to B$), there exists a unique continuous map $r\colon(\widetilde{B},\tilde{b}_0)\to(E,e_0)$ with $q\circ r = p$.

Step 2 — $r$ is surjective. Let $e\in E$. Since $q$ is surjective and $E$ is connected, choose a path $\alpha$ in $E$ from $e_0$ to $e$. Then $q\circ\alpha$ is a path in $B$ from $b_0$ to $q(e)$. Lift $q\circ\alpha$ via $p$ to a path $\tilde{\alpha}$ in $\widetilde{B}$ starting at $\tilde{b}_0$. Then $q(r(\tilde{\alpha}(t))) = p(\tilde{\alpha}(t)) = q(\alpha(t))$ for all $t$, and both $r\circ\tilde{\alpha}$ and $\alpha$ start at $e_0$, so by the Unique Lifting Property (for the covering $q$) we get $r\circ\tilde{\alpha} = \alpha$. In particular $r(\tilde{\alpha}(1)) = \alpha(1) = e$.

Step 3 — $r$ is a covering map. Let $e\in E$ and set $b = q(e)\in B$. Choose an evenly covered neighborhood $U$ of $b$ for both $p$ and $q$ (intersect two such neighborhoods if necessary). Write $$p^{-1}(U) = \bigsqcup_\alpha \widetilde{V}_\alpha, \qquad q^{-1}(U) = \bigsqcup_\beta W_\beta$$ where each $\widetilde{V}_\alpha$ maps homeomorphically onto $U$ via $p$, and each $W_\beta$ maps homeomorphically onto $U$ via $q$.

For any sheet $\widetilde{V}_\alpha$: since $q\circ r = p$ and $p|_{\widetilde{V}_\alpha}\colon\widetilde{V}_\alpha\to U$ is a homeomorphism, we have $r(\widetilde{V}_\alpha)\subseteq q^{-1}(U) = \bigsqcup W_\beta$. Since $\widetilde{V}_\alpha$ is connected, $r(\widetilde{V}_\alpha)$ lies in a single sheet $W_\beta$. Now on $\widetilde{V}_\alpha$: $$r|_{\widetilde{V}_\alpha} = (q|_{W_\beta})^{-1}\circ p|_{\widetilde{V}_\alpha}\colon \widetilde{V}_\alpha \xrightarrow{\;\cong\;} U \xrightarrow{\;\cong\;} W_\beta$$ which is a composition of homeomorphisms, hence a homeomorphism onto $W_\beta$.

Conversely, fix the sheet $W_\beta$ containing $e$. The preimage $r^{-1}(W_\beta)$ is a union of sheets $\widetilde{V}_\alpha$: precisely those $\widetilde{V}_\alpha$ that $r$ maps into $W_\beta$. We just showed each such $r|_{\widetilde{V}_\alpha}\colon\widetilde{V}_\alpha\to W_\beta$ is a homeomorphism, and the $\widetilde{V}_\alpha$'s are disjoint. So $W_\beta$ is evenly covered by $r$, proving $r$ is a covering map. □

3.2   Existence of the Universal Cover

Hypothesis: Semi-Locally Simply Connected

A space $B$ is semi-locally simply connected if every point $b\in B$ has a neighborhood $U$ such that the inclusion-induced map $\pi_1(U,b)\to\pi_1(B,b)$ is trivial: every loop in $U$ is null-homotopic in $B$ (not necessarily in $U$).

This is the precise condition needed for the universal cover to exist. All manifolds and CW complexes satisfy it. But not every space does — see the example below.

Non-Example: The Hawaiian Earring

The Hawaiian earring is the subspace of $\mathbb{R}^2$ defined by $$\mathbb{H} = \bigcup_{n=1}^{\infty} C_n, \qquad C_n = \bigl\{(x,y)\in\mathbb{R}^2 : (x-\tfrac{1}{n})^2+y^2 = \tfrac{1}{n^2}\bigr\},$$ i.e., the union of circles of radius $1/n$ centered at $(1/n,0)$, all passing through the origin $\mathbf{0}$.

This space is not semi-locally simply connected at the origin. Indeed, let $U$ be any neighborhood of $\mathbf{0}$ in $\mathbb{H}$. Since the circles $C_n$ shrink to $\mathbf{0}$, for all sufficiently large $n$ the entire circle $C_n$ lies inside $U$. The loop going once around $C_n$ lies in $U$, but it is not null-homotopic in $\mathbb{H}$ (intuitively, it "wraps around the $n$-th circle" and cannot be contracted past it). So $\pi_1(U,\mathbf{0})\to\pi_1(\mathbb{H},\mathbf{0})$ is not trivial, no matter how small $U$ is.

Consequently, the Hawaiian earring has no universal cover. Its fundamental group is an enormously complicated (uncountably generated) group — very different from the free group $F_\infty$ that one might naively expect by analogy with finite wedges of circles.

Why This Condition is Needed

In the construction of the universal cover (below), we define $\widetilde{B}$ as a space of homotopy classes of paths from $b_0$. To put a topology on $\widetilde{B}$ that makes $p$ a covering map, we need the basic open sets $\langle[\gamma],U\rangle$ to depend only on the homotopy class of the path $\eta$ within $U$. This requires that loops in $U$ that are null-homotopic in $B$ do not create distinct points in $\widetilde{B}$ — which is exactly what semi-local simple connectedness guarantees.

Without this condition, the fiber over a point would not be discrete, and the "covering" would fail to be a covering map.

The Hawaiian earring $\mathbb{H}$: circles $C_n$ of radius $1/n$ all passing through the origin. Any neighborhood $U$ of the origin (dashed) contains infinitely many entire circles, each carrying a non-trivial loop. So $\mathbb{H}$ is not semi-locally simply connected at $\mathbf{0}$.
Theorem (Existence of the Universal Cover)

Let $B$ be path-connected, locally path-connected, and semi-locally simply connected. Then there exists a universal covering $p\colon\widetilde{B}\to B$, unique up to isomorphism of covering spaces.

Construction

Fix a basepoint $b_0\in B$. As a set, define $$\widetilde{B} \;=\; \bigl\{[\gamma] : \gamma \text{ is a path in } B \text{ from } b_0 \text{ to some point } b\bigr\},$$ where $[\gamma]$ denotes the homotopy class of $\gamma$ rel endpoints.

The projection. Define $p([\gamma]) = \gamma(1)$ (the endpoint of $\gamma$). This is well-defined since homotopic paths rel endpoints share the same endpoint.

Topology. For an open set $U\subseteq B$ that is path-connected and whose loops are null-homotopic in $B$, and for a class $[\gamma]$ with $\gamma(1)\in U$, define $$\langle [\gamma], U\rangle \;=\; \bigl\{[\gamma*\eta] : \eta \text{ is a path in } U \text{ starting at } \gamma(1)\bigr\}.$$ These sets form a basis for a topology on $\widetilde{B}$.

Key verifications:

(i) $p$ is continuous and maps each $\langle[\gamma],U\rangle$ homeomorphically onto $U$.

(ii) $p^{-1}(U) = \bigsqcup_{[\gamma]} \langle[\gamma],U\rangle$ where $[\gamma]$ ranges over distinct homotopy classes of paths ending in $U$, giving the evenly covered property.

(iii) $\widetilde{B}$ is simply connected: a loop $\tilde\alpha$ in $\widetilde{B}$ based at $[c_{b_0}]$ projects to a loop $\alpha = p\circ\tilde\alpha$ in $B$. The lift $\tilde\alpha$ returns to $[c_{b_0}]$, which means $[\alpha]$ is trivial in $\pi_1(B,b_0)$. But the endpoint of $\tilde\alpha$ is exactly $[\alpha]$, so $[\alpha]=[c_{b_0}]$. Hence $\alpha$ is null-homotopic, so $\tilde\alpha$ is null-homotopic by lifting. □

Each point of $\mathbb{R}$ corresponds to a homotopy class of paths from $1\in S^1$: the integer part counts how many times the path winds around, and the fractional part records where the endpoint is. 3.3   The Fundamental Group Acts on the Universal Cover

Theorem

Let $p\colon\widetilde{B}\to B$ be the universal covering. The deck transformation group $\mathrm{Deck}(\widetilde{B}/B)$ is isomorphic to $\pi_1(B,b_0)$.

Proof

Fix a basepoint $\tilde{b}_0\in p^{-1}(b_0)$. For each $[\alpha]\in\pi_1(B,b_0)$, define $\phi_{[\alpha]}\colon\widetilde{B}\to\widetilde{B}$ as follows: for $\tilde{b}\in\widetilde{B}$, let $\tilde\sigma$ be a path from $\tilde{b}_0$ to $\tilde{b}$ in $\widetilde{B}$. Set $\sigma = p\circ\tilde\sigma$ (a path in $B$ from $b_0$ to $p(\tilde{b})$). Then define $\phi_{[\alpha]}(\tilde{b})$ to be the endpoint of the lift of $\alpha*\sigma$ starting at $\tilde{b}_0$.

Well-defined: since $\widetilde{B}$ is simply connected, $\tilde\sigma$ is unique up to homotopy rel endpoints, so $[\sigma]$ depends only on $\tilde{b}$, and the construction depends only on the homotopy class $[\alpha]$ (by the homotopy lifting corollary).

Deck transformation: $p\circ\phi_{[\alpha]}(\tilde{b}) = (p\circ\text{lift of }\alpha*\sigma)(1) = (\alpha*\sigma)(1) = \sigma(1) = p(\tilde{b})$, so $p\circ\phi_{[\alpha]}=p$.

Homomorphism: $\phi_{[\alpha]}\circ\phi_{[\beta]}(\tilde{b})$ corresponds to prepending $\alpha$ then $\beta$, which is the lift of $\alpha*\beta*\sigma$, so $\phi_{[\alpha]}\circ\phi_{[\beta]} = \phi_{[\alpha*\beta]} = \phi_{[\alpha]\cdot[\beta]}$.

Bijective: $\phi_{[\alpha]}(\tilde{b}_0)$ is the endpoint of the lift of $\alpha$ starting at $\tilde{b}_0$. Different $[\alpha]$ give different endpoints (by the homotopy lifting corollary), so $[\alpha]\mapsto\phi_{[\alpha]}$ is injective. It is surjective because every deck transformation is determined by where it sends $\tilde{b}_0$ (uniqueness), and every point in $p^{-1}(b_0)$ is the endpoint of some lifted loop. □

The Quotient Identification

The action of $\pi_1(B,b_0)$ on $\widetilde{B}$ is free (no fixed points) and properly discontinuous. It follows that $$B \;\cong\; \widetilde{B}\,\big/\,\pi_1(B,b_0).$$ This is the precise sense in which $B$ is "determined" by its universal cover and its fundamental group.

3.4   Classification of Coverings (Galois Correspondence)

Theorem (Classification of Coverings)

Let $B$ be path-connected, locally path-connected, and semi-locally simply connected, with basepoint $b_0$. There is a bijection $$\left\{\begin{array}{c}\text{Connected covering spaces}\\p\colon(E,e_0)\to(B,b_0)\\\text{(up to isomorphism)}\end{array}\right\} \;\;\longleftrightarrow\;\; \left\{\begin{array}{c}\text{Subgroups}\\ H\le\pi_1(B,b_0)\end{array}\right\}$$ given by $p\mapsto p_*(\pi_1(E,e_0))$. The correspondence reverses inclusions: a larger subgroup $H$ corresponds to a "smaller" (fewer sheets) covering.

Special cases

$H = \{e\}$ (trivial subgroup): corresponds to the universal cover $\widetilde{B}\to B$. This has the most sheets (equal to $|\pi_1(B)|$ when finite).

$H = \pi_1(B,b_0)$ (full group): corresponds to the trivial covering $\mathrm{id}\colon B\to B$. This has $1$ sheet.

$H = n\mathbb{Z}\le\mathbb{Z}\cong\pi_1(S^1)$: corresponds to the $n$-fold covering $S^1\xrightarrow{z^n}S^1$.

3.5   Back to Poincaré

Closing the Circle

We can now see why the Poincaré conjecture is so powerful. Let $M$ be any compact 3-manifold.

By covering space theory, $M$ has a universal cover $\widetilde{M}$, which is simply connected, and $M\cong\widetilde{M}/\pi_1(M)$.

If $\widetilde{M}$ is compact (which happens when $\pi_1(M)$ is finite), then $\widetilde{M}$ is a compact, simply connected 3-manifold, and the Poincaré conjecture tells us $\widetilde{M}\cong S^3$.

Therefore: every compact 3-manifold with finite fundamental group is a quotient $S^3/\Gamma$ for some finite group $\Gamma$ acting freely on $S^3$. The classification of such manifolds reduces to the classification of finite subgroups of $\mathrm{SO}(4)$ acting freely on $S^3$, a concrete algebraic problem.

More broadly, covering space theory tells us that topology (at the level of coverings) is equivalent to algebra (subgroups of the fundamental group). This bridge between geometry and algebra is one of the most beautiful and productive ideas in mathematics.
