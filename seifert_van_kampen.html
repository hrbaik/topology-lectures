0. Algebraic Preliminaries: Free Products

0.1   The Free Product of Groups

Definition: Free Product

Let $G$ and $H$ be groups. A word in $G$ and $H$ is a finite sequence $$g_1 h_1 g_2 h_2 \cdots g_n h_n$$ where each $g_i\in G$ and $h_i\in H$ (allowing $g_i = e_G$ or $h_i = e_H$). A word is reduced if:

(i) No $g_i$ (except possibly $g_1$ or $g_n$ at the ends) is $e_G$, and similarly for $h_i$.

(ii) Elements from the same group are not adjacent (i.e., the letters alternate strictly between $G$ and $H$).

The free product $G * H$ is the set of all reduced words, with the group operation given by concatenation followed by reduction (combining adjacent letters from the same group, and cancelling identities).

Universal Property

The free product $G*H$ satisfies the following universal property. There are natural inclusion homomorphisms $\iota_G\colon G\hookrightarrow G*H$ and $\iota_H\colon H\hookrightarrow G*H$. For any group $K$ and any homomorphisms $\varphi\colon G\to K$, $\psi\colon H\to K$, there exists a unique homomorphism $\Phi\colon G*H\to K$ such that $\Phi\circ\iota_G = \varphi$ and $\Phi\circ\iota_H = \psi$.

Concretely, $\Phi$ acts on a reduced word by applying $\varphi$ to each $G$-letter and $\psi$ to each $H$-letter, then multiplying in $K$: $$\Phi(g_1 h_1 g_2 h_2\cdots) = \varphi(g_1)\,\psi(h_1)\,\varphi(g_2)\,\psi(h_2)\cdots$$

Example

$\mathbb{Z}*\mathbb{Z}$ is the free group on two generators $F_2$. If we denote the generator of the first copy by $a$ and the second by $b$, then elements are reduced words in $a, a^{-1}, b, b^{-1}$, such as $a^2 b^{-1} a b^3$. This group is non-abelian: $ab\ne ba$.

Left: An unreduced word is simplified by combining adjacent letters from the same group and removing identities. Right: Multiplying two reduced words — concatenate, then reduce the junction.

0.2   The Free Product with Amalgamation

Definition: Amalgamated Free Product

Let $G$, $H$ be groups, $C$ a group, and $i\colon C\to G$, $j\colon C\to H$ homomorphisms. The amalgamated free product (or pushout) is the quotient $$G *_C H \;=\; (G * H)\, \big/\, N$$ where $N$ is the normal closure of $\bigl\{ i(c)\,j(c)^{-1} : c\in C \bigr\}$.

In other words, $G *_C H$ is the free product $G*H$ with the additional relations that identify $i(c)$ with $j(c)$ for every $c\in C$.

Universal Property of the Pushout

$G *_C H$ satisfies the following universal property. For any group $K$ and homomorphisms $\varphi\colon G\to K$, $\psi\colon H\to K$ such that $\varphi\circ i = \psi\circ j$ (i.e., they agree on the image of $C$), there exists a unique homomorphism $\Phi\colon G*_C H\to K$ with $\Phi|_G = \varphi$ and $\Phi|_H = \psi$.

Figure 0.2 — Universal Property of the Amalgamated Free Product
The amalgamated free product $G*_C H$ is the "most general" group that receives homomorphisms from both $G$ and $H$ agreeing on $C$. Every such pair of homomorphisms factors uniquely through $G*_C H$.
Key Special Case

When $C = \{e\}$ is trivial (so $i$ and $j$ are the trivial homomorphisms), the amalgamated free product reduces to the ordinary free product: $G *_{\{e\}} H = G * H$. No identifications are imposed.

1. The Seifert–van Kampen Theorem

1.1   Setup

The Situation

Let $X$ be a topological space, and suppose $X = A\cup B$ where $A$ and $B$ are open subsets with $A$, $B$, and $A\cap B$ all path-connected. Choose a basepoint $x_0\in A\cap B$.

The inclusions induce a commutative diagram of fundamental groups:

$\pi_1(A\cap B,x_0) \xrightarrow{i_*} \pi_1(A,x_0)$,   $\pi_1(A\cap B,x_0) \xrightarrow{j_*} \pi_1(B,x_0)$

where $i\colon A\cap B\hookrightarrow A$ and $j\colon A\cap B\hookrightarrow B$ are the inclusion maps.

Figure 1.1 — The Decomposition $X = A\cup B$
$X$ is decomposed as the union of two open sets $A$ and $B$. Their intersection $A\cap B$ must be path-connected. The inclusions $A\cap B\hookrightarrow A$ and $A\cap B\hookrightarrow B$ induce homomorphisms on fundamental groups.

1.2   Statement

Theorem (Seifert–van Kampen)

Let $X = A\cup B$ with $A$, $B$ open in $X$, and $A$, $B$, $A\cap B$ all path-connected. Fix a basepoint $x_0\in A\cap B$. Then the homomorphism $$\Phi\colon \pi_1(A)*_{\pi_1(A\cap B)}\pi_1(B) \;\longrightarrow\; \pi_1(X)$$ induced by the inclusions $A\hookrightarrow X$ and $B\hookrightarrow X$ is an isomorphism.

Equivalently: $\pi_1(X,x_0)$ is the pushout of $\pi_1(A,x_0) \xleftarrow{i_*} \pi_1(A\cap B,x_0) \xrightarrow{j_*} \pi_1(B,x_0)$ in the category of groups.

Unpacking the Statement

Concretely, the theorem says two things:

(1) Generation. Every loop in $X$ based at $x_0$ is homotopic to a product of loops, each lying entirely in $A$ or entirely in $B$. (Surjectivity of $\Phi$.)

(2) Relations. The only relations among these generators, beyond those already holding in $\pi_1(A)$ and $\pi_1(B)$ individually, come from the intersection: if $\gamma$ is a loop in $A\cap B$, then its class viewed in $\pi_1(A)$ equals its class viewed in $\pi_1(B)$ inside $\pi_1(X)$. (Injectivity of $\Phi$, i.e., the kernel is exactly the amalgamation relations.)

Important Special Case: Simply Connected Intersection

If $A\cap B$ is simply connected, then $\pi_1(A\cap B) = \{e\}$, and the amalgamated free product reduces to the ordinary free product: $$\pi_1(X) \;\cong\; \pi_1(A) * \pi_1(B).$$ This is the simplest and most frequently used case.

1.3   Proof

Proof

Step 0 — Defining $\Phi$. By the universal property of the amalgamated free product, the inclusion-induced homomorphisms $\pi_1(A)\to\pi_1(X)$ and $\pi_1(B)\to\pi_1(X)$ (which agree on $\pi_1(A\cap B)$ since both composites equal the inclusion $A\cap B\hookrightarrow X$) give a unique homomorphism $$\Phi\colon \pi_1(A)*_{\pi_1(A\cap B)}\pi_1(B) \;\longrightarrow\; \pi_1(X,x_0).$$ On a word $\alpha_1\beta_1\alpha_2\beta_2\cdots$ (with $\alpha_k\in\pi_1(A)$ and $\beta_k\in\pi_1(B)$), $\Phi$ sends it to $[\alpha_1*\beta_1*\alpha_2*\beta_2*\cdots]$ viewed as a loop in $X$. We must prove $\Phi$ is both surjective and injective.

Step 1 — Surjectivity. Let $\gamma\colon [0,1]\to X$ be a loop at $x_0$. We must show that $[\gamma]$ lies in the image of $\Phi$, i.e., $\gamma$ is homotopic to a product of loops each in $A$ or $B$.

Since $\{A,B\}$ is an open cover of $X$ and $[0,1]$ is compact, the Lebesgue number lemma gives a partition $$0 = t_0 \lt t_1 \lt \cdots \lt t_n = 1$$ such that each $\gamma([t_{k-1},t_k])$ lies entirely in $A$ or entirely in $B$.

Each subdivision point $\gamma(t_k)$ lies in $A$ or $B$ (or both). If $\gamma(t_k)\in A\cap B$, we can connect it to $x_0$ by a path in $A\cap B$ (using path-connectedness of $A\cap B$). If $\gamma(t_k)$ lies in only one of $A$ or $B$, then the two adjacent segments $\gamma|_{[t_{k-1},t_k]}$ and $\gamma|_{[t_k,t_{k+1}]}$ both lie in the same set (say $A$), so we can merge them into a single segment in $A$. After such merging, we may assume every subdivision point $\gamma(t_k)$ lies in $A\cap B$.

Now for each $k$, choose a path $\sigma_k$ in $A\cap B$ from $x_0$ to $\gamma(t_k)$ (with $\sigma_0=\sigma_n=$ constant at $x_0$ since $\gamma(0)=\gamma(1)=x_0$). Define $$\lambda_k \;=\; \sigma_{k-1} * \gamma|_{[t_{k-1},t_k]} * \bar{\sigma}_k.$$ Each $\lambda_k$ is a loop at $x_0$ lying entirely in $A$ or entirely in $B$ (since $\gamma|_{[t_{k-1},t_k]}$ lies in one of them, and $\sigma_{k-1}$, $\sigma_k$ lie in $A\cap B\subseteq$ both). Furthermore, $$\gamma \;\simeq\; \lambda_1 * \lambda_2 * \cdots * \lambda_n$$ because the connecting paths $\bar{\sigma}_k * \sigma_k$ are each homotopic to the constant path. So $[\gamma] = \Phi([\lambda_1][\lambda_2]\cdots[\lambda_n])$, proving surjectivity.

Step 2 — Injectivity. This is the heart of the proof. We must show: if $\alpha_1\beta_1\cdots\alpha_m\beta_m$ is a word in $\pi_1(A)*\pi_1(B)$ with $\Phi(\alpha_1\beta_1\cdots\alpha_m\beta_m) = [c_{x_0}]$ in $\pi_1(X)$, then the word is trivial in $\pi_1(A)*_{\pi_1(A\cap B)}\pi_1(B)$.

Write each $\alpha_k$ and $\beta_k$ as homotopy classes of actual loops: $\alpha_k = [a_k]$ with $a_k$ a loop in $A$, and $\beta_k = [b_k]$ with $b_k$ a loop in $B$. The hypothesis says the concatenation $$\gamma = a_1*b_1*a_2*b_2*\cdots*a_m*b_m$$ is null-homotopic in $X$. Let $H\colon[0,1]^2\to X$ be a null-homotopy, i.e., $H(s,0)=\gamma(s)$, $H(s,1)=x_0$, $H(0,t)=H(1,t)=x_0$ for all $s,t$.

Step 2a (Grid). Since $\{A,B\}$ is an open cover of $X$ and $[0,1]^2$ is compact, the Lebesgue number lemma gives $\delta \gt 0$ such that every subsquare of side $\le\delta$ is mapped by $H$ into $A$ or $B$. Choose a grid on $[0,1]^2$ with mesh $\lt\delta$. We can further refine this grid so that the bottom edge $[0,1]\times\{0\}$ has vertical grid lines at each junction point $t_k$ where $\gamma$ transitions between $a_k$, $b_k$, etc.

Step 2b (Vertex paths). Each vertex $v$ of the grid maps to a point $H(v)\in X$. If $H(v)\in A\cap B$, choose a path $\sigma_v$ in $A\cap B$ from $x_0$ to $H(v)$. If $H(v)$ lies in only one of $A$ or $B$, the two rectangles sharing $v$ must both map into the same set (by our choice of $\delta$), so we can handle this by a small refinement. After possible refinement, assume every vertex maps to $A\cap B$ (or use paths in whichever set contains the vertex).

Step 2c (Edge loops). For each edge $e$ of the grid from vertex $v$ to vertex $w$, define the loop $$\lambda_e = \sigma_v * H|_e * \bar{\sigma}_w,$$ which is a loop at $x_0$ lying entirely in $A$ or entirely in $B$ (since $H(e)$ lies in one of them).

Step 2d (Rectangle relations). Consider any small rectangle $R$ of the grid. Its four edges $e_{\text{bot}}$, $e_{\text{right}}$, $e_{\text{top}}$, $e_{\text{left}}$ form a cycle. Since $H(R)$ lies in a single set ($A$ or $B$), the boundary loop is null-homotopic within that set. This gives: $$[\lambda_{e_{\text{bot}}}]\,[\lambda_{e_{\text{right}}}]\,[\lambda_{\bar{e}_{\text{top}}}]\,[\lambda_{\bar{e}_{\text{left}}}] \;=\; e \quad\text{in } \pi_1(A) \text{ or } \pi_1(B).$$ In other words, each rectangle gives a relation that holds within $\pi_1(A)$ or $\pi_1(B)$.

Step 2e (Telescoping). Now consider the product of all edge-loops along the bottom row of the grid: this represents $[\gamma] = [\alpha_1][\beta_1]\cdots[\alpha_m][\beta_m]$. The top, left, and right edges of the grid map to $x_0$ (since $H(s,1)=x_0$ and $H(0,t)=H(1,t)=x_0$), so their edge-loops are trivial.

Using the rectangle relations from Step 2d, we can "push" the bottom row upward through the grid, one row at a time. Each rectangle replaces a bottom-edge loop with top/side-edge loops using a relation that holds in $\pi_1(A)$ or $\pi_1(B)$. If an edge lies in $A\cap B$, the corresponding edge-loop lies in $\pi_1(A\cap B)$, and moving it between the $A$ and $B$ factors is exactly the amalgamation relation $i_*(c) = j_*(c)$.

After pushing all the way to the top row (where everything is trivial), we have shown that the original word $[\alpha_1][\beta_1]\cdots[\alpha_m][\beta_m]$ can be reduced to the identity using only: (i) relations within $\pi_1(A)$ or $\pi_1(B)$, and (ii) the amalgamation relations $i_*(c) = j_*(c)$ for $c\in\pi_1(A\cap B)$. These are precisely the relations in $\pi_1(A)*_{\pi_1(A\cap B)}\pi_1(B)$. So the word is trivial there. □

2. Applications

2.0   Preliminary: Deformation Retractions

Definition: Retraction and Deformation Retraction

Let $A\subseteq X$ be a subspace. A retraction of $X$ onto $A$ is a continuous map $r\colon X\to A$ such that $r(a) = a$ for all $a\in A$ (i.e., $r\circ\iota = \mathrm{id}_A$, where $\iota\colon A\hookrightarrow X$ is the inclusion).

A deformation retraction of $X$ onto $A$ is a continuous map $H\colon X\times[0,1]\to X$ such that:

(i) $H(x,0) = x$ for all $x\in X$  (starts as the identity),

(ii) $H(x,1)\in A$ for all $x\in X$  (ends in $A$),

(iii) $H(a,t) = a$ for all $a\in A$ and all $t\in[0,1]$  (points of $A$ stay fixed throughout).

In other words, $H$ is a homotopy from $\mathrm{id}_X$ to a retraction $r = H(\cdot,1)$, and this homotopy keeps $A$ fixed at all times. We say $A$ is a deformation retract of $X$.

Proposition

If $A$ is a deformation retract of $X$ and $x_0\in A$, then the inclusion $\iota\colon A\hookrightarrow X$ induces an isomorphism $$\iota_*\colon \pi_1(A,x_0) \;\xrightarrow{\;\cong\;}\; \pi_1(X,x_0).$$

Proof

Let $r\colon X\to A$ be the retraction $r(x) = H(x,1)$. Since $r\circ\iota = \mathrm{id}_A$, we have $r_*\circ\iota_* = \mathrm{id}_{\pi_1(A)}$. In particular, $\iota_*$ is injective and $r_*$ is surjective.

It remains to show $\iota_*\circ r_* = \mathrm{id}_{\pi_1(X)}$, i.e., $\iota\circ r \simeq \mathrm{id}_X$ as maps $X\to X$. But this is exactly what the deformation retraction $H$ provides: $H(\cdot,0) = \mathrm{id}_X$ and $H(\cdot,1) = \iota\circ r$, so $\iota\circ r\simeq \mathrm{id}_X$.

For any loop $\gamma$ in $X$ at $x_0$, the map $t\mapsto H(\gamma(\cdot),t)$ gives a homotopy from $\gamma$ to $\iota\circ r\circ\gamma$ (which is a loop in $A$ viewed in $X$). Moreover, since $H(x_0,t) = x_0$ for all $t$ (condition (iii), as $x_0\in A$), this homotopy fixes the basepoint. Therefore $[\gamma] = \iota_*([r\circ\gamma])$, showing $\iota_*\circ r_* = \mathrm{id}_{\pi_1(X)}$.

Thus $\iota_*$ and $r_*$ are mutual inverses. □

Why This Matters

This result lets us replace a complicated space $X$ with a simpler subspace $A$ (its deformation retract) when computing fundamental groups. We will use this repeatedly in the applications below: to identify $\pi_1$ of an open set, we deformation-retract it onto a simpler space whose $\pi_1$ we already know.

2.1   Wedge of Circles: $\pi_1(S^1\vee S^1)\cong \mathbb{Z}*\mathbb{Z}$

Proposition

The fundamental group of the wedge of two circles is the free group on two generators: $$\pi_1(S^1\vee S^1) \;\cong\; \mathbb{Z}*\mathbb{Z} \;=\; F_2.$$

Proof

Let $X = S^1\vee S^1$, the one-point union at $x_0$. Let $A$ be the union of the first circle and a small open arc of the second circle containing $x_0$, and let $B$ be the union of the second circle and a small open arc of the first circle containing $x_0$.

Then $A$ and $B$ are open in $X$, both path-connected, and $A\cap B$ is a small "cross" shape centered at $x_0$, which is contractible and hence simply connected.

Now $A$ deformation-retracts onto $S^1$ (the first circle), so $\pi_1(A)\cong\mathbb{Z}$. Similarly $\pi_1(B)\cong\mathbb{Z}$. Since $A\cap B$ is simply connected, Seifert–van Kampen gives: $$\pi_1(X) \;\cong\; \pi_1(A)*\pi_1(B) \;\cong\; \mathbb{Z}*\mathbb{Z}. \quad\square$$

Generalization

By induction, the fundamental group of a wedge of $n$ circles is the free group on $n$ generators: $$\pi_1\bigl(\textstyle\bigvee_{k=1}^n S^1\bigr) \cong F_n = \underbrace{\mathbb{Z}*\mathbb{Z}*\cdots*\mathbb{Z}}_n.$$

Figure 2.1 — Wedge of Circles: $S^1\vee S^1$
The wedge $S^1\vee S^1$ is two circles joined at a point $x_0$. The open sets $A$ (blue) and $B$ (purple) each contain one full circle plus a small arc of the other, so that $A\cap B$ (green) is contractible.

2.2   Higher Spheres: $\pi_1(S^n) = \{e\}$ for $n\ge 2$

Proposition

For $n\ge 2$, the sphere $S^n$ is simply connected: $\pi_1(S^n)=\{e\}$.

Proof

Write $S^n = A\cup B$ where $A = S^n\setminus\{N\}$ (remove the north pole) and $B = S^n\setminus\{S\}$ (remove the south pole).

Both $A$ and $B$ are homeomorphic to $\mathbb{R}^n$ via stereographic projection, so $\pi_1(A)=\pi_1(B)=\{e\}$.

The intersection $A\cap B = S^n\setminus\{N,S\}$ is homeomorphic to $\mathbb{R}^n\setminus\{0\}\cong S^{n-1}\times\mathbb{R}$. For $n\ge 2$, this is path-connected (since $S^{n-1}$ is path-connected for $n-1\ge 1$).

By Seifert–van Kampen: $$\pi_1(S^n) \cong \pi_1(A)*_{\pi_1(A\cap B)}\pi_1(B) = \{e\}*_{\pi_1(A\cap B)}\{e\} = \{e\}. \quad\square$$

Why $n\ge 2$?

For $n=1$, the intersection $A\cap B = S^1\setminus\{N,S\}$ consists of two disjoint open arcs, which is not path-connected. So the hypothesis of the theorem fails, and indeed $\pi_1(S^1)\cong\mathbb{Z}\ne\{e\}$ (as we computed using covering spaces).

2.3   Orientable Surfaces

Proposition

Let $\Sigma_g$ be the closed orientable surface of genus $g\ge 1$ (the $g$-holed torus). Then $$\pi_1(\Sigma_g) \;\cong\; \bigl\langle a_1,b_1,\ldots,a_g,b_g \;\big|\; [a_1,b_1][a_2,b_2]\cdots[a_g,b_g]=1 \bigr\rangle$$ where $[a_i,b_i] = a_i b_i a_i^{-1} b_i^{-1}$ is the commutator.

Proof

Realize $\Sigma_g$ as a quotient of a $4g$-gon $P$ by the identification $a_1 b_1 a_1^{-1}b_1^{-1}a_2 b_2 a_2^{-1}b_2^{-1}\cdots a_g b_g a_g^{-1}b_g^{-1}$ on the boundary.

Let $x_0$ be the single vertex (all vertices of $P$ are identified to one point). Let $A$ be the image of the interior of $P$ together with a small strip along the boundary — concretely, $A = \Sigma_g\setminus\{x_0\}$. Let $B$ be a small open disk around $x_0$ in $\Sigma_g$.

Then $B$ is contractible, so $\pi_1(B) = \{e\}$, and the Seifert–van Kampen theorem gives $$\pi_1(\Sigma_g) \cong \pi_1(A)*_{\pi_1(A\cap B)}\{e\} \cong \pi_1(A)/N$$ where $N$ is the normal closure of $\mathrm{Im}(j_*\colon \pi_1(A\cap B)\to\pi_1(A))$.

Now $A = \Sigma_g\setminus\{x_0\}$ deformation-retracts onto the $1$-skeleton, which is a wedge of $2g$ circles: $\pi_1(A)\cong F_{2g} = \langle a_1,b_1,\ldots,a_g,b_g\rangle$.

The intersection $A\cap B$ is an annulus (a punctured disk), with $\pi_1(A\cap B)\cong\mathbb{Z}$ generated by a small loop around $x_0$. The map $j_*$ sends this generator to the boundary word $[a_1,b_1]\cdots[a_g,b_g]\in\pi_1(A)$ (this is the single loop formed by the boundary of the polygon, which becomes a loop around the missing point $x_0$).

Since $j_*$ is trivial into $\pi_1(B)=\{e\}$, the amalgamation kills $j_*(\text{generator})$, giving $$\pi_1(\Sigma_g) \cong F_{2g}/\langle\!\langle [a_1,b_1]\cdots[a_g,b_g]\rangle\!\rangle = \langle a_1,b_1,\ldots,a_g,b_g \mid [a_1,b_1]\cdots[a_g,b_g]=1\rangle. \quad\square$$

Genus $g$ = 1
The orientable surface $\Sigma_g$ is obtained by identifying the edges of a $4g$-gon. The boundary word $a_1 b_1 a_1^{-1}b_1^{-1}\cdots a_g b_g a_g^{-1}b_g^{-1}$ becomes the single relation in $\pi_1(\Sigma_g)$.

2.4   Attaching Cells

General Principle

The Seifert–van Kampen theorem is especially powerful for CW complexes. To build a space cell by cell:

$0$-cells and $1$-cells: Each $1$-cell attached to a discrete set of $0$-cells contributes a generator. A maximal tree in the $1$-skeleton contributes trivially. So $\pi_1(X^{(1)})$ is a free group, with one generator for each $1$-cell not in a chosen maximal tree.

$2$-cells: Each $2$-cell is a disk attached along its boundary circle $S^1\to X^{(1)}$. By SVK (with $A = X^{(1)}\cup(\text{open $2$-cell})$ minus a point in the interior, and $B =$ open $2$-cell), each $2$-cell contributes one relation: the attaching map read as a word in the generators.

Higher cells ($n\ge 3$): Attaching $n$-cells for $n\ge 3$ does not change $\pi_1$ (since $S^{n-1}$ is simply connected for $n\ge 3$).

3. Classification of Closed Orientable Surfaces

3.1   The Classification Theorem

Theorem (Classification of Closed Orientable Surfaces)

Every closed (= compact, without boundary) connected orientable surface is homeomorphic to exactly one of the following: $$S^2,\; \Sigma_1,\; \Sigma_2,\; \Sigma_3,\; \ldots$$ where $\Sigma_g$ is the orientable surface of genus $g$ (the connected sum of $g$ copies of the torus $T^2$), and $\Sigma_0 = S^2$.

The non-negative integer $g$ is called the genus and is a complete topological invariant: $\Sigma_g\cong\Sigma_h$ if and only if $g = h$.

Proof Strategy (Outline)

The proof proceeds in two stages. First, one shows that every closed orientable surface admits a triangulation, and any triangulated surface can be reduced to one of the standard polygon presentations $a_1 b_1 a_1^{-1}b_1^{-1}\cdots a_g b_g a_g^{-1}b_g^{-1}$ by a sequence of combinatorial moves (cutting and pasting along edges). This shows existence of the normal form.

Second, one must show that surfaces with different genus are genuinely non-homeomorphic. This is where the fundamental group enters: it provides a computable invariant that distinguishes them.

3.2   Fundamental Groups of Surfaces (Recap)

Summary of $\pi_1(\Sigma_g)$

From Section 2.3, we computed using the Seifert–van Kampen theorem: $$\pi_1(\Sigma_0) = \pi_1(S^2) = \{e\}, \qquad \pi_1(\Sigma_1) = \pi_1(T^2) \cong \mathbb{Z}^2,$$ $$\pi_1(\Sigma_g) \cong \bigl\langle a_1,b_1,\ldots,a_g,b_g \;\big|\; [a_1,b_1]\cdots[a_g,b_g]=1\bigr\rangle \quad(g\ge 1).$$

Note that $\pi_1(\Sigma_1)$ is abelian ($\mathbb{Z}^2$), but $\pi_1(\Sigma_g)$ is non-abelian for $g\ge 2$.

3.3   The Abelianization Distinguishes Genus

Definition: Abelianization

For any group $G$, the abelianization is the quotient $G^{\mathrm{ab}} = G/[G,G]$, where $[G,G]$ is the commutator subgroup (the normal subgroup generated by all commutators $ghg^{-1}h^{-1}$). This is the largest abelian quotient of $G$.

Key property: any homomorphism from $G$ to an abelian group factors through $G^{\mathrm{ab}}$.

Proposition

The abelianization of $\pi_1(\Sigma_g)$ is $\mathbb{Z}^{2g}$: $$\pi_1(\Sigma_g)^{\mathrm{ab}} \;\cong\; \mathbb{Z}^{2g}.$$

Proof

The abelianization of a group with presentation $\langle S \mid R \rangle$ is obtained by adding the relations $[s,t]=1$ for all generators $s,t\in S$ (i.e., making all generators commute). So: $$\pi_1(\Sigma_g)^{\mathrm{ab}} = \bigl\langle a_1,b_1,\ldots,a_g,b_g \;\big|\; [a_1,b_1]\cdots[a_g,b_g]=1,\;\text{all generators commute}\bigr\rangle.$$

Once all generators commute, $[a_i,b_i] = a_i b_i a_i^{-1}b_i^{-1} = e$ for each $i$, so the single relation $[a_1,b_1]\cdots[a_g,b_g]=1$ becomes $e\cdots e = 1$, which is automatically satisfied. The abelianization is therefore the free abelian group on $2g$ generators: $$\pi_1(\Sigma_g)^{\mathrm{ab}} \cong \mathbb{Z}^{2g}. \quad\square$$

Remark: This is the First Homology

The abelianization $\pi_1(X)^{\mathrm{ab}}$ is naturally isomorphic to the first homology group $H_1(X;\mathbb{Z})$. So $H_1(\Sigma_g;\mathbb{Z})\cong\mathbb{Z}^{2g}$. The number $2g$ is called the first Betti number $b_1(\Sigma_g)$.

3.4   The Fundamental Group Classifies Surfaces

Theorem

Let $\Sigma_g$ and $\Sigma_h$ be closed orientable surfaces. Then the following are equivalent:

(i) $\Sigma_g$ is homeomorphic to $\Sigma_h$.

(ii) $\pi_1(\Sigma_g)\cong\pi_1(\Sigma_h)$.

(iii) $g = h$.

Proof

(i) $\Rightarrow$ (ii): This is immediate: homeomorphic spaces have isomorphic fundamental groups.

(ii) $\Rightarrow$ (iii): If $\pi_1(\Sigma_g)\cong\pi_1(\Sigma_h)$, then their abelianizations are isomorphic: $\mathbb{Z}^{2g}\cong\mathbb{Z}^{2h}$. A free abelian group $\mathbb{Z}^n$ has rank $n$, and the rank is an isomorphism invariant (for instance, $\mathbb{Z}^n\otimes_{\mathbb{Z}}\mathbb{Q}\cong\mathbb{Q}^n$ as a $\mathbb{Q}$-vector space, and vector space dimension is an invariant). Therefore $2g = 2h$, hence $g = h$.

(iii) $\Rightarrow$ (i): This is the content of the Classification Theorem: there is, up to homeomorphism, exactly one closed orientable surface of genus $g$. □

Why This Is Remarkable

In general, the fundamental group does not determine a space up to homeomorphism. For instance, $S^2\times S^1$ and $\mathbb{R}P^3\;\#\;\mathbb{R}P^3$ are non-homeomorphic 3-manifolds that share the same fundamental group. In higher dimensions the situation is even worse.

The fact that $\pi_1$ alone classifies closed orientable surfaces is a special feature of dimension $2$. It reflects the fact that surfaces have very constrained topology: they are completely determined by a single integer (the genus), and the fundamental group is rich enough to detect it.

3.5   The Euler Characteristic

Definition: Euler Characteristic

For a surface $\Sigma$ with a triangulation having $V$ vertices, $E$ edges, and $F$ faces, the Euler characteristic is $$\chi(\Sigma) = V - E + F.$$ This is independent of the choice of triangulation.

Proposition

For the orientable surface $\Sigma_g$: $\chi(\Sigma_g) = 2 - 2g.$

Proof

From the polygon presentation, $\Sigma_g$ has a CW structure with $1$ vertex (all corners identified), $2g$ edges (the $a_i, b_i$), and $1$ face (the $4g$-gon itself). So $$\chi(\Sigma_g) = 1 - 2g + 1 = 2 - 2g. \quad\square$$

Alternative Detection of Genus

So the genus $g$ can also be read off from the Euler characteristic: $g = (2-\chi)/2$. For a triangulated surface, $\chi$ can be computed directly by counting simplices. This gives a second (and more elementary) proof that surfaces of different genus are non-homeomorphic, without using the fundamental group at all.

However, the fundamental group approach is more powerful in several ways: it applies to non-orientable surfaces as well, it interacts with covering space theory, and it generalizes to higher-dimensional manifolds (even though it is no longer a complete invariant there).
